# DreamRunner Art Direction

## North Star

DreamRunner is not a fantasy world.

It is a world where ordinary matter occasionally becomes transparent.

The game should feel like running through a vast, quiet, self-generating landscape where most moments are calm, sparse, and physical — but every once in a while, the world aligns in a way that feels magical, ancient, and true.

The goal is not constant beauty.

The goal is revelation.

DreamRunner should create moments where the player feels small, awake, mortal, and free.

## Core Art Direction Statement

DreamRunner is a procedural pilgrimage through ordinary matter under extraordinary alignments.

The world is not trying to show a perfect realm. It is trying to show glimpses of one.

The player should feel that magic is not inside a single object. Magic happens when scale, weather, silence, distance, sky, ground, motion, and placement briefly become correct together.

## Emotional Target

The player should feel:

- dreamy
- hazy
- ethereal
- timeless
- solitary
- free
- small against the world
- occasionally awakened
- occasionally lost in scale
- occasionally touched by something beyond explanation

The world should sometimes feel like:

- a 4 AM roadside stop during a long journey
- a cold wind outside a gas station
- a mountain ridge after hours of hiking
- a cloudy sky suddenly revealing its massive scale
- a quiet field where one object feels strangely important
- a dream remembered only in fragments

The emotional goal is not comfort or spectacle.

The goal is a rare feeling of alchemy.

## What “Alchemy” Means Here

Alchemy in DreamRunner means the transformation of ordinary elements into a moment of revelation.

A tree is not magical by itself.

A rock is not magical by itself.

A beam of light is not magical by itself.

But a lonely tree on a ridge, under a heavy sky, with fog below it and silence around it, can become magical.

The game should not rely on decorative fantasy. It should rely on arrangement.

Magic is composition.

## The World Should Open

The game world should feel mostly grounded, quiet, and traversable.

But occasionally, the world should “open.”

An opening is a moment where the player feels that the landscape has revealed something deeper than its physical parts.

Examples:

- cresting a hill and seeing a distant dream signal in a huge valley
- running through fog and finding a solitary giant tree
- seeing a massive sky dwarf the landscape
- reaching a dream marker at the exact moment light, haze, and scale align
- discovering a strange human trace in an otherwise vast natural space
- watching the horizon dissolve into a pale atmospheric distance

These moments should not happen constantly.

If everything is magical all the time, nothing is magical.

## Visual Pillars

### 1. Scale Must Humble the Player

Scale is one of the main sources of awe.

The player should often feel small compared to the sky, the land, and rare monumental forms.

Use:

- huge skies
- low horizons
- distant fog
- large cloud masses
- rare giant trees
- rare monumental stones
- distant rings, pillars, arches, or beams
- vast empty plains
- large silhouettes on ridges

Avoid filling the world with too many medium-sized objects.

Medium-scale clutter kills awe.

The scale language should be:

- small player
- sparse ordinary objects
- occasional enormous forms

### 2. Sparse, Not Empty

The world should have space, silence, and distance.

It should not feel crowded.

However, it must not feel unfinished or empty.

Sparse means every object has room to matter.

Empty means nothing has meaning.

Prefer:

- one giant tree on a ridge
- a few rocks arranged like a strange marker
- a lone structure in the distance
- a single beam beyond a valley
- small clusters separated by large open running space

Avoid:

- evenly scattered props everywhere
- dense forests
- random clutter
- visual noise
- arcade obstacle courses

### 3. Haze Is Essential

Haze gives the world memory, distance, and longing.

The far world should often be suggested rather than fully shown.

Use:

- atmospheric fog
- soft horizon fade
- dusty air
- pale distance colors
- low contrast in far terrain
- subtle particle motes
- washed sky gradients

Avoid:

- overly sharp infinite visibility
- heavy horror fog
- noisy particle effects
- thick fog that prevents navigation

The world should feel remembered, not photographed.

### 4. The Sky Is a Character

The sky is not background.

The sky is one of the main emotional systems of DreamRunner.

The same terrain should feel completely different under different sky moods.

Possible sky states:

- pale morning openness
- washed afternoon stillness
- golden edge light
- cold overcast heaviness
- twilight ache
- 4 AM pre-dawn blue
- deep dream night
- grey-violet liminal haze
- cloudy mass that makes the player feel tiny

Avoid relying on one permanently pretty sky.

The sky should create emotional weather.

### 5. The Sublime and the Banal Must Coexist

Transcendence should not come only from obviously beautiful things.

Sometimes magic comes from ordinary, ugly, or human things placed correctly.

DreamRunner may eventually include limited “human trace” elements:

- lonely shelter frames
- bus-stop-like structures
- concrete slabs
- broken sign frames
- old poles
- distant lights
- abandoned platforms
- quiet shrine-like forms
- radio-tower silhouettes
- strange utilitarian objects with no explanation

These should be rare.

They should not turn the world into a town, survival game, or post-apocalyptic setting.

They should feel like traces, not settlements.

The tension between the cosmic and the banal is important.

### 6. Magic Must Be Episodic

The game should not be visually intense all the time.

Moments of revelation need contrast.

Use long stretches of:

- quiet traversal
- simple terrain
- soft atmosphere
- ordinary props
- silence
- distant signals

Then occasionally create:

- a reveal
- a ridge moment
- a strange landmark
- a giant form
- a sky transition
- a perfect alignment of light and distance

Magic should feel discovered, not spammed.

## Style Direction

DreamRunner should use stylized atmospheric realism.

It should not be photorealistic.

It should not be cartoonish.

It should not become generic fantasy.

It should not look like a toy world.

The style should use:

- simple readable forms
- soft silhouettes
- restrained materials
- strong atmospheric depth
- elegant primitive geometry
- large-scale composition
- low visual noise
- carefully controlled color

The current primitive/low-poly foundation is acceptable, but it should evolve toward poetic simplicity rather than cute simplicity.

The target is not “pretty low-poly forest.”

The target is poetic procedural landscape.

## Color Direction

The color palette should feel faded, breathable, and emotionally loaded.

Prefer:

- soft sage greens
- dusty olive grass
- pale warm earth
- muted stone grey
- washed blue
- cold pre-dawn blue
- dusty violet
- faint pink horizon light
- pale gold during rare revelation moments
- desaturated browns
- atmospheric blue-grey distance

Avoid:

- bright arcade colors
- neon fantasy colors everywhere
- oversaturated grass green
- high-contrast cartoon materials
- generic fantasy purple everywhere
- excessive glow

Glow should be sacred and controlled.

The dream signal can glow, but the whole world should not glow constantly.

## Shape Language

### Terrain

Terrain should feel soft, rolling, and traversable.

Use:

- broad hills
- long slopes
- ridges
- valleys
- open plains
- gentle terrain rhythm

Avoid:

- noisy terrain
- jagged cliffs unless intentionally rare
- cramped paths
- obstacle-course layouts
- terrain that constantly interrupts flow

The landscape should support running, jumping, and gliding.

### Trees

Trees should be stylized and simple, but with stronger scale variation.

Use:

- small trees
- medium trees
- tall trees
- rare giant solitary trees
- asymmetric silhouettes
- occasional clusters
- enough spacing for sprinting

Avoid:

- identical tree scale everywhere
- dense forests
- trees that feel like decoration only
- tiny toy-like trees dominating the landscape

Trees should sometimes become landmarks.

### Rocks

Rocks should vary from small stones to large boulders.

Use:

- small scattered stones
- medium rocks
- large boulders
- rare monumental stones
- clusters that feel intentional
- silhouettes visible from distance

Avoid:

- many tiny pebble props
- uniform scale
- collision traps
- cluttered obstacle fields

### Landmarks

Landmarks should be rare, visible, and compositionally important.

Possible landmark types:

- dream rings
- tall pale pillars
- giant stone forms
- tilted monoliths
- solitary giant trees
- floating slabs
- arch-like structures
- far beams
- liminal human traces
- strange shelters
- horizon frames

Landmarks should not feel like collectible icons.

They should feel like things the world has revealed.

## Dream Signal Direction

The dream signal is not just a waypoint.

It is the first version of the game’s pilgrimage structure.

It should feel like a call.

The signal should be:

- visible from far away
- minimal
- dreamlike
- not too UI-heavy
- integrated with the landscape
- capable of creating ridge reveals and distant goals

The off-screen indicator is useful, but it should remain subtle.

The player should feel guided, not managed.

## Composition Rules

Future procedural systems should not only spawn objects.

They should create compositions.

Important composition patterns:

### Ridge Reveal

The player crests a hill and sees a large view:
- distant signal
- valley
- haze
- large sky
- one or two major silhouettes

### Solitary Giant

A rare giant object appears in an otherwise open area:
- giant tree
- giant stone
- tall pillar
- lonely arch

The point is scale and loneliness.

### Liminal Clearing

A quiet space with few objects:
- soft fog
- low sound
- a ring or marker
- sparse rocks or trees
- open sky

### Human Trace

A strange ordinary structure appears in a vast world:
- shelter
- slab
- frame
- pole
- platform
- light

It should feel emotionally ambiguous.

### Horizon Call

Something visible very far away:
- beam
- ring
- large silhouette
- massive cloud break

The player should want to run toward it.

## Dimension Layers, Not Weather Presets

The current mood states should be treated as prototypes for parallel dimensions or layers of reality, not simply sunset, night, fog, or weather presets.

The terrain may remain physically similar while its meaning, atmosphere, objects, and emotional tone change. The world should feel like one place seen through multiple states of being.

`Q` dimension shifting is the current prototype version of this idea. It proves layered contrast; it is not the final dimension-shifting system.

## Same World, Different Reality

The same hill, road, tree, ruin, signal, or landmark may reveal a different truth in each layer.

A monolith in one layer might be a ruin, tower, shadow, absence, or memory in another. A road may disappear but leave lights behind. A shelter may look abandoned in one reality and recently occupied in another.

These changes must not feel like random reskins. They should feel like hidden truths becoming visible.

## Passive Storytelling

Story should be uncovered through environment, repetition, transformation, distance, sound, symbols, and absence.

Prefer environmental traces:

- roads
- shelters
- utility poles and dead power lines
- abandoned stations
- ruins
- repeated symbols
- distant lights
- objects that change or vanish between dimensions

Avoid exposition-heavy dialogue, lore dumps, and dependence on cutscenes. The player should feel they are discovering a story, not being told one.

## Human Traces and Liminal Memory

DreamRunner should not drift into generic fantasy. Sparse human traces can give the world specificity and memory:

- lonely roadside structures
- old travel shelters
- dead power lines
- tiny lights at impossible distances
- bus-station / 4 AM travel energy
- abandoned markers of passage

These traces should remain rare and meaningful. They are evidence, not clutter; passage, not settlement.

## Possible Dimension Identities

These are conceptual examples, not final names or a required set:

- **Waking / Pale World:** breathable, almost real, before revelation.
- **Dead / Empty World:** colder, stripped, silent, abandoned.
- **Memory / Golden World:** warm, nostalgic, marked by human traces, lights, and lost roads.
- **Liminal Night / Dream-Between:** darker, sacred, watched, and impossibly large.
- **Forgotten Road / Dust World:** dry, lonely, wind-worn, with roadside remnants.

## Dimension Design Implications

The current mood system may later evolve into dimension shifting.

Early implementation should stay simple: sky, fog, palette, light, and audio. Later passes may add dimension-specific object visibility, landmark transformations, sound changes, story fragments, and gameplay consequences.

Do not overbuild this early. The current MVP remains focused on movement, vastness, gliding, signals, scale, atmosphere, and browser performance.

Across every layer, preserve the existing philosophy:

- magic is episodic, not constant
- magic is composition, not decoration
- ordinary matter becomes transparent
- sparse, not empty
- scale must humble the player
- avoid generic fantasy and clutter
- avoid neon magical effects everywhere

## Revelation Events

A future system may create rare “revelation events.”

These should be environmental and compositional, not traditional game objectives.

Possible triggers:

- player has traveled for a while
- player reaches a dream signal
- player crests a hill
- player enters a clearing
- player moves far from start
- sky changes slowly
- a rare landmark is generated

Possible effects:

- subtle sky shift
- sound drop
- distant landmark appears
- signal becomes framed by terrain
- fog thins
- light warms briefly
- giant form appears in the distance

Revelation events should be rare.

They should feel earned by movement.

## Sound Direction

Sound is critical to the art direction.

The game should not use constant emotional music.

Sound should feel like weather, memory, and omen.

Future sound principles:

- preserve silence
- use wind as a core sound
- use distant low drones sparingly
- use soft environmental tones
- use rare musical fragments
- use footsteps and movement as grounding
- allow sound to thin out during revelation moments
- avoid constant soundtrack pressure

Silence is part of the sacred feeling.

Do not fill every moment.

## UI Direction

The UI should be minimal and quiet.

The player should not feel like they are inside a dashboard.

Use:

- subtle text
- soft opacity
- simple hints
- minimal indicators
- debug information hidden behind toggle

Avoid:

- bright arcade UI
- excessive labels
- minimaps unless absolutely necessary
- constant objective spam
- loud icons

Normal UI should feel like a whisper.

Debug UI can remain practical for development.

## Things To Avoid

Avoid these directions:

- generic fantasy forest
- cute toy-like low-poly world
- constant glowing magic
- dense obstacle course
- endless collectibles
- arcade scoring as the central identity
- overdesigned UI
- neon cyberdream aesthetic
- photorealistic terrain
- horror fog
- constant music
- too many landmarks
- too many medium-sized props
- clutter replacing composition
- spectacle replacing revelation

## Current Visual Problem

The current prototype works mechanically but is still visually early.

Current issues:

- world can feel too empty
- trees and rocks can still feel too small or toy-like
- sky is not emotionally rich enough yet
- terrain lacks strong compositional reveals
- atmosphere exists but does not yet fully control mood
- landmarks are still basic
- the world does not yet create enough moments of scale-based awakening

This is expected for the current stage.

The next visual work should focus on art direction systems, not asset polish.

## Implementation Priorities

### Priority 1: Preserve Flow

The game is about running, jumping, and gliding.

Do not add visual systems that harm movement flow.

### Priority 2: Add Scale

Introduce rare large forms and stronger distance silhouettes.

### Priority 3: Add Mood States

Create atmosphere states that change sky, fog, lighting, and particles.

### Priority 4: Add Compositional Landmarks

Move from random props to arranged moments.

### Priority 5: Add Human Trace Carefully

Only after the natural sublime is working, add rare banal/liminal structures.

### Priority 6: Add Sound

Sound should be added as soon as the core visual mood is stable.

## Guidance For Codex

When editing visual systems, Codex should follow these principles:

1. Do not make the world busier just to make it feel alive.
2. Prefer fewer, stronger compositions.
3. Preserve open space for sprinting and gliding.
4. Use scale variation aggressively but rarely.
5. Keep the sky and haze emotionally important.
6. Use glow sparingly.
7. Avoid generic fantasy visuals.
8. Keep browser performance safe.
9. Keep Game.tscn as the main playable scene.
10. Do not break existing movement, camera, mouse capture, dream signal, debug HUD, or browser export.

## Short Version

DreamRunner should feel like a quiet procedural pilgrimage through a landscape that is often ordinary, sometimes strange, and occasionally transcendent.

The world should not constantly show magic.

It should sometimes arrange itself so that magic becomes visible.
